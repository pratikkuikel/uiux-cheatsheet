10 Rules That Fix 90% of UI Problems

Each rule includes the practical why ‚Äî so when you enforce them you'll know what pain they prevent.

1
Use an 8px spacing system.
Reason: predictable spacing creates rhythm. Humans detect patterns instantly ‚Äî irregular spacing feels "off" even if they can't name why. Enforce via tokens and utility spacing.
2
Reduce everything until nothing breaks.
Reason: every UI element adds cognitive load. Removing non-essential elements speeds task completion and lowers support tickets.
3
One primary color, one accent, neutral greys.
Reason: too many colors destroy visual hierarchy and brand recognition. A tight palette guides attention to actions and status.
4
Everything must have hierarchy.
Reason: the brain scans pages top‚Üídown, left‚Üíright. Proper hierarchy reduces time to understand a screen and prevents feature competition.
5
Buttons: only 3 types (Primary, Secondary, Destructive).
Reason: too many button styles dilute importance. One primary per screen prevents accidental flows and clarifies calls-to-action.
6
Align everything.
Reason: alignment forms invisible grids. Misalignment reduces perceived polish and can break reading rhythm.
7
Clear labels over clever labels.
Reason: ambiguous copy requires interpretation time. Clear labels reduce mistakes and improve discoverability.
8
Group related items using whitespace.
Reason: whitespace acts like punctuation ‚Äî it defines relationships and improves scanability.
9
Micro-interactions matter.
Reason: feedback confirms actions, lowers anxiety, and increases perceived speed and quality.
10
Consistency = perceived quality.
Reason: consistent components reduce learning time, bugs, and cognitive friction ‚Äî users form muscle memory faster.

UX Laws ‚Äî and why they influence your product

A
Jakob's Law
Users prefer familiar patterns. If you align with known interfaces (Slack, Stripe, Notion) you reduce the learning curve. In practice: prefer established patterns for core tasks.
B
Hick's Law
More choices = slower decisions. Reduce options for critical flows (checkout, setup) or use progressive disclosure.
C
Fitts' Law
Make common actions large and easy to hit. This is crucial for mobile/touch targets and primary CTAs.
D
Miller's Law
People can keep ~7¬±2 items in working memory. Use grouping and chunking to reduce load (tabs, sections).
E
Peak-End Rule
People judge experiences by peak moments and the ending. Make onboarding and success flows delightful ‚Äî they stick.
F
Doherty Threshold
If the system responds in <400ms it feels instantaneous. Use optimistic updates, skeletons, and progressive loading to hit the threshold.
G
Law of Proximity
Items placed close together are perceived as related. Use spacing to show relationships instead of extra lines or borders.

Layout Patterns That Always Work

Use predictable structures so users can rely on their mental models rather than relearn layout per module.

1
Classic SaaS Layout
Header ‚Üí Sidebar ‚Üí Content ‚Üí Optional Right Panel. It scales across POS, dashboards and settings.
2
Card-Based Grouping
Cards are composable, responsive, and easy to scan ‚Äî use them for lists, metric tiles, and related data.
3
Single Primary Action per Screen
It reduces friction and accidental navigation; secondary actions should be clearly secondary in hierarchy.

Color Strategy

1
Primary, Accent, Neutrals
A disciplined palette preserves hierarchy and readability. Reserve bright accent for attention points only.
2
Semantic Colors
Use color meaningfully: green=success, red=error, yellow=warning. It speeds recognition across cultures (with exceptions ‚Äî test for context).
3
Soft Blacks & Whites
Avoid #000 and #FFF as pure extremes‚Äîuse soft greys for comfortable reading and accessible contrast.

Practical UI/UX Process

  1. Define the problem ‚Äî a screen must solve a clear user job.
  2. Map user flows ‚Äî list steps and decisions.
  3. Wireframe (low-fidelity) ‚Äî validate structure before pixels.
  4. Design (high-fidelity) ‚Äî apply tokens, spacing, and components.
  5. Prototype & test ‚Äî 5 users reveal ~80% of issues.
  6. Ship & iterate ‚Äî measure and refine. Perfection is the enemy of shipping.
Why: This process forces you to fail cheap and catch foundational problems early, saving engineering time.

Batuly-specific UX Tips

  • Keep POS & eCommerce flows consistent ‚Äî users switch between them in the same session.
  • Use wizards for setup (domain, payments, inventory) ‚Äî reduce choice at each step.

Accessibility ‚Äî non-negotiable

1
Contrast
Aim for WCAG AA minimum (4.5:1 for normal text). Good contrast improves readability for everyone.
2
Keyboard & Focus
Users must be able to use the app without a mouse. Visible focus states help keyboard users.
3
Touch Targets
At least 44x44px for tappable elements to avoid frustration on mobile devices.
